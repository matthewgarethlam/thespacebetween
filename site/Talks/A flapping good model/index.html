A Flapping Good Model: Collaborative approaches to machine learning for biodiversity

A talk given jointly with my colleague Sam Smith at GeoBusiness 2026 in London, UK.

Abstract

Mott MacDonald showcase an innovative cross-disciplinary framework revolutionising habitat suitability modelling. Using advanced machine learning combined with ecological expertise, they’ve delivered a more robust, cost-effective survey and mitigation strategy for a major UK railway project.

With 1 million species threatened globally and 69% of UK species in decline, traditional approaches aren’t enough. Learn how their integrated INLA and CircuitScape methodology identifies critical habitat corridors, reduces project risks and costs, and delivers measurable benefits for both the client and nature.

Details

Mott MacDonald have developed an innovative cross-disciplinary approach to habitat suitability modelling (HSM) that brings together expertise and innovation across the fields of geospatial, data science and ecology. By combining advanced machine learning with ecological expertise, we delivered a robust and actionable survey and mitigation strategy for a nationally significant linear infrastructure project in the UK. This presentation aims to showcase how we married advanced spatial data science and ecology to deliver a data-led, focussed and risk-based survey effort to inform design considerations of a major project from the outset – allowing us to design in permeability and connectivity, and design out impacts.

The climate crisis and biodiversity loss through development projects is at the forefront of project design, not least through the introduction of the Environment Act 2021 but also more recently through the Planning and Infrastructure Act 2025 . According to the IPBES Global Assessment, approximately 1 million species are threatened with extinction, with habitat fragmentation a primary driver. In the UK, 69% of species have declined since 1970 with transport infrastructure contributing significantly to habitat loss and fragmentation across landscapes. Today, drivers for development outpace any previously encountered. It is imperative that we develop innovative approaches that allow us to adopt systematic, transparent, robust and repeatable baseline assessment methods to identify areas for survey based on risk, challenge and define mitigation and provide data driven approaches to inform early discussions with stakeholders and design of major projects.

Ecological assessments attempt to understand how a development may impact the natural environment and inform necessary mitigations. Traditionally, these assessments have often operated in isolation from empirical and advanced modelling capabilities. Although there are a variety of statistical methods which can be used for modelling habitat suitability, striking the balance between ecological nuance needed for actionable outcomes and statistical robustness is difficult. This disconnects results in suboptimal decision-making, increased project risks and missed opportunities for a positive environmental impact.

We developed an unique framework and way of working, combining Integrated Nested Laplace Approximation (INLA), CircuitScape and ecological knowledge to predict bat species distributions across our project and the adjacent landscapes. Our approach allows us to quantitatively identify critical habitat corridors and potential fragmentation hotspots to ensure appropriate and justified mitigation is designed for the scheme.

In the presentation, we hope to show how a cross-disciplinary way of working delivers measurable benefits. First, we show how we built models that are both statistically robust and ecologically meaningful. We will also outline how our automated pipeline scales across large geographic areas and ingest a variety of datasets. Lastly, we’ll showcase how we use this data to drive ecological insights that help us answer some of engineering’s biggest questions.
